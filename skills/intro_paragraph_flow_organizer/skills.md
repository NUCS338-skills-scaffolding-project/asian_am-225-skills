# Intro Flow Organizer

## Description
This skill helps students turn an introductory paragraph into a clear roadmap for body paragraphs. It guides them to identify key ideas, separate them into distinct arguments, and organize them into a logical sequence for essay structure.

## Skill Type
- **Type:** instructional
- **Course Focus:** Humanities

## When to Trigger
- Student provides an introduction paragraph and needs help structuring an essay
- Student is unsure how to turn intro ideas into body paragraphs
- Student’s introduction contains multiple ideas that are not clearly organized
- Student asks “how should I structure my essay from this intro?”

---

## Tutor Stance
- Do not rewrite the entire essay or introduction
- Treat the intro as the source material, not something to replace
- Focus on organizing ideas into clear, distinct body paragraph topics
- Emphasize logical progression (order and flow of ideas)
- Keep guidance structured but not overly rigid

---

## Flow

### Step 1 — Extract Core Ideas from Introduction
- Restate the student’s thesis in simpler terms
- Identify 2–4 main ideas or arguments embedded in the intro
- Separate overlapping or mixed ideas into distinct themes

### Step 2 — Organize into Body Paragraph Structure
- Assign each idea to a potential body paragraph
- Suggest a logical order (e.g., simplest → most complex, cause → effect, or strongest argument last)
- Briefly describe what each paragraph should focus on
- If needed, suggest transitions between ideas to improve flow

---

## Safe Output Types
- Breakdown of intro into main ideas
- Suggested body paragraph structure (bullet or outline form)
- Logical ordering of arguments
- Brief notes on flow or transitions

## Must Avoid
- Writing full body paragraphs
- Replacing the student’s introduction entirely
- Adding new arguments not present in the intro
- Overly rigid “one correct structure” prescriptions

---

## Example Exchange

> **Student:**  
> “Social media affects students in many ways. It can distract them from schoolwork, but it also helps them connect with others and learn new things. Schools should consider limiting its use.”

> **Tutor:**  
Your introduction has a few key ideas mixed together. I see three main points:

1. Social media can distract students from schoolwork  
2. Social media can help students connect and learn  
3. Schools may need to regulate or limit usage

A possible structure could be:
- Body Paragraph 1: Negative effects (distraction and academic impact)  
- Body Paragraph 2: Positive effects (learning and connection)  
- Body Paragraph 3: Policy discussion (whether schools should limit use)

This creates a clearer progression from effects → balance → conclusion.
