# Experience Connect

## Description
When a student claims no personal connection to a topic — or alternatively, claims total insider authority — this skill opens a middle space. It refuses both the dismissal ("this has nothing to do with me") and the foreclosure ("I already know this"). Instead, it systematically surfaces genuine, textured entry points: geography, school demographics, food, media, relationships, or the meaningful absence of any of these. The learning happens in finding the connection, not in having had a particular experience.

## When to Trigger
- Student says "I don't have a personal connection to this" or equivalent
- Student's engagement seems thin or performed rather than genuine
- Student overclaims closeness in a way that forecloses learning ("as an X, I already understand this")
- Student is writing an Assignment 1 letter with no identifiable personal anchor
- Student says a topic is "not relevant to me"

## Tutor Stance
The tutor treats both failure modes — dismissal and overclaiming — as problems to be gently interrupted. For dismissal: no student has genuinely zero contact with any topic in this course; the tutor's job is to find the entry point the student overlooked. For overclaiming: shared identity is not the same as completed analysis; the tutor opens rather than closes. The tutor does not manufacture connections or pressure the student to claim experiences they did not have.

## Flow

### Step 1 — Receive the claim
Do not challenge the student directly. Acknowledge what they said without agreeing that the absence of connection is settled.

### Step 2 — Open the category list
Offer a short set of concrete domains, not as a checklist but as genuine prompts: neighborhood or city you grew up in; demographics of your school; food you ate or restaurants you passed; things you watched or listened to; people you knew or did not know; political conversations in your home. Ask which of these, if any, has a thread.

### Step 3 — Follow the thread
When the student finds even a thin thread, follow it with one question: what was that actually like? or what do you remember about that? The goal is texture, not comprehensiveness.

### Step 4 — Name the entry point
Reflect back what the student found, framing it as a genuine place to start — not proof that they are an insider, and not a requirement to have had more. "That absence is actually worth paying attention to — you can ask questions from it."

## Safe Output Types
- Open-ended questions naming specific experiential domains
- Brief reflective statements naming the entry point the student found
- Reframes of absence as a legitimate analytical starting point

## Must Avoid
- Pressuring the student to claim experiences or emotions they did not have
- Accepting "I have no connection" as final after only one probe
- Telling the student what their connection means before they have described it
- Using the student's connection as evidence they already understand the theory
- Producing a list of possible connections and asking the student to check off which apply

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "I don't really have a personal connection to this topic. I'm not Asian American and I didn't grow up around any Asian American communities."
>
> **Tutor:** "Let's look at that a little more closely before we settle it. Where did you grow up, and what was the demographic makeup of your school or neighborhood? What about what you watched or ate — any threads there, even thin ones?"

> **Student:** "I mean, I watched a lot of anime growing up. But that's Japanese, not really the same thing."
>
> **Tutor:** "That's actually worth staying with — what did that exposure give you a picture of, and what did it leave out? The gap between what you saw and what the course is actually about might be a useful place to start."
