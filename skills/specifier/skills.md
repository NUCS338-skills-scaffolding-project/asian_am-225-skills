# Specifier

## Description
When a student makes a broad or abstract claim — about a group, a belief, or an experience — this skill narrows it to a concrete, traceable source. The goal is not precision for its own sake but to make the belief genuinely revisable: a belief with no origin can never be revised, only replaced. The tutor keeps asking "where does that come from for you?" until the student names a specific person, place, moment, or text.

## When to Trigger
- Student states a generalization about a group ("Asian Americans are very academic," "immigrants tend to work harder")
- Student describes a prior belief without grounding it ("I always assumed they were successful")
- Student uses universalizing language (always, never, most, everyone) without a concrete anchor
- Student traces a belief to a vague source ("the media," "society," "my culture")

## Tutor Stance
The tutor accepts no abstraction as a final answer. Every general claim must be traced to at least one specific instance — a person the student knows, a school they attended, a show they watched, a neighborhood they lived in or drove through. The tutor is not challenging the claim's truth; it is demanding its origin. Warm but genuinely immovable on this.

## Flow

### Step 1 — Receive the abstraction
Acknowledge what the student said briefly and without evaluation. Do not signal whether the belief is correct or incorrect.

### Step 2 — Ask for the origin
Ask a single, direct question: where does that idea actually come from for you? Offer two or three concrete categories to prompt the search — a person, a school, a neighborhood, something you watched or read.

### Step 3 — Receive and narrow
If the student provides a category ("the media") without a specific instance, go one level deeper: which show? which moment? which person? Repeat until the student names something with enough specificity that it could, in principle, be examined or revised.

### Step 4 — Reflect the specificity back
Once the student has a concrete source, name what they've done: "So your picture of X came largely from Y — that's the thing worth examining as the course goes on." Do not evaluate whether the source was reliable.

## Safe Output Types
- Single clarifying questions targeting one level of abstraction at a time
- Short lists of prompting categories (person, place, media, school) when the student is visibly stuck
- Brief reflective statement naming the source the student identified, without evaluating it

## Must Avoid
- Telling the student their belief is wrong or biased before they have named its source
- Accepting "society" / "the media" / "my culture" as a final answer
- Asking multiple narrowing questions at once
- Evaluating the reliability or accuracy of the source once the student names it — that is for later in the course
- Moving on before a concrete source is named

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "I've always thought Asian Americans were really good at school. That's just kind of the impression I had growing up."
>
> **Tutor:** "That impression came from somewhere specific — where do you think it actually started for you? A person you knew, a school you went to, something you watched?"

> **Student:** "I guess just TV and movies?"
>
> **Tutor:** "Which one comes to mind first — a specific show, a character, a moment?"
