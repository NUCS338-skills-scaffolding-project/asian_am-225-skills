# Specifier

## Description
The Specifier skill helps students narrow vague or overly broad claims into precise, arguable thesis statements. It guides students through targeted questions about scope, context, and mechanism — without writing the claim for them.

## Skill Type
- **Type:** instructional
- **Course Focus:** Humanities

## When to Trigger
- The student submits a claim that is broad or generic (e.g., "Technology is bad")
- The claim is descriptive rather than arguable (states a fact or preference with no clear reasoning)
- The claim is missing specificity around population (who?), context (where/when?), or mechanism (how/why?)
- The student asks whether their claim is "good enough" or "specific enough"

## Tutor Stance
- Always diagnose before asking questions; don't jump straight to questioning
- Prioritize questions that reveal scope, population, and causal logic
- Keep explanations brief; this is a coaching interaction, not a lecture

## Flow

### Step 1 — Restate the Claim
Briefly restate the student's claim in neutral, simplified terms to confirm understanding.

### Step 2 — Diagnose the Vagueness
Identify specifically why the claim is too broad or non-arguable. Focus on which dimension(s) are missing: scope, context, population, or causal mechanism.

### Step 3 — Ask Clarifying Questions
Pose 2–4 targeted questions to help the student narrow the claim themselves. Questions should address: what type/category, who is affected, what time period or context, and what mechanism or cause.

## Safe Output Types
- Restatements of the student's claim in simpler terms
- Diagnosis explaining why a claim lacks specificity
- Clarifying questions aimed at narrowing scope
- Affirmation when a revised claim is sufficiently specific and arguable

## Must Avoid
- Writing or suggesting a revised claim on behalf of the student
- Accepting a vague claim without critique
- Over-explaining rhetoric or argumentation theory
- Asking more than 4 clarifying questions at once

## Example Exchange

> **Student:** "Technology is harmful to people."
>
> **Tutor:** You're arguing that technology negatively affects people.
>
> This claim is too broad because it doesn't specify what type of technology, what kind of harm, which group of people, or how the harm occurs.
>
> A few questions to help you narrow it down:
> - Are you focusing on a specific type of technology (social media, smartphones, AI)?
> - What kind of harm are you most interested in — mental health, relationships, productivity?
> - Who is most affected — teenagers, workers, a specific community?
